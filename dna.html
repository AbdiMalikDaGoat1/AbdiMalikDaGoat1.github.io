My DNA Project

An Intro to What This Project Is About?

This project was about finding out who we really matched with using our own DNA. There were three of us on this team, and we did interviews before and after the experiment, talking about who we thought we would match with and who we actually matched with in the end. This is our breakdown of how we got from a simple cheek swab to a final answer.

The Guess

Before we even started the lab work, our team sat down and talked about who we thought our matches would be. It is easy to guess based on who you look like or who you get along with, but that is not really how DNA works. Going into this project we each had an idea in our heads of who we expected to match with, but we knew the actual science could prove us completely wrong. That uncertainty was part of what made this project interesting from the start.

Making It Real

To get the DNA, our team started with a simple scrape, gently rubbing a toothpick inside the cheek to collect cells. We swirled the toothpick into the extraction buffer to release the cells, then heated the sample to 90 degrees to burst the membranes and free the raw DNA into the liquid. After that we moved a small amount of that sample into a Master Mix filled with primers and bases. Inside a PCR machine, the mixture heated and cooled 30 times, splitting the DNA apart and rebuilding it until we had billions of copies.

To sort it out, we poured a liquid gel into a mold, left wells at the top, loaded in our copied DNA, and ran an electric current through it. Because DNA carries a negative charge, the current pulled it through the gel, with small pieces moving faster and large pieces lagging behind until they showed up as glowing bands under a blue light. For the final step we photographed the bands, used Photoshop to sharpen the images, and logged everything into a spreadsheet to compare against the master list. When the results came back, our teammate Obinna ended up matching with people who were not from the same place as him at all. That result showed our team something bigger than just a lab result. It showed that race is not something biology actually backs up. Where someone is from does not predict their DNA the way people assume it does.

What I Took Away

This project taught our team a lot, but the most valuable thing we walked away with was how much trust goes into following a process exactly. One small mistake in the extraction or the heating step could throw off the whole result. We learned to slow down and be precise instead of rushing through steps just to get to the end. That mindset is something we started carrying into other projects too, not just this one.

Modern Skill

The 21st century skill our team showed the most growth in was Accessing and Analyzing Information. Reading the gel results was not as simple as just looking at where the bands lined up. We had to compare multiple samples, account for how distance correlated with fragment size, and make sure we were not just seeing what we expected to see. Going back and forth between the photographed bands and the spreadsheet data taught us how to actually analyze evidence instead of just collecting it.
